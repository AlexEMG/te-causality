About

Source code for the Transfer Entropy (TE) based measure of effective connectivity (called Generalized Transfer Entropy, or GTE) published in Model-free Reconstruction of Neuronal Network Connectivity from Calcium Imaging Signals (pre-print).

Extract effective connectivities based on spike times, have it simulate fluorescence signals for you, or supply your own signal. As TE is model-independent, virtually any kind of signal can be used.

Features

  • Extract effective connectivities based on real or simulated fluorescence signals.
  • Model-free, so you can supply any other kind of signal.
  • Computationally efficient.
  • Can auto-detect the optimal bin number and/or the optimal conditioning level.
  • Easy parameter control via the SimKernel package.
  • Actively maintained by a growing group of active users.

What's included?

Primarily, we provide the GTE analysis in a program called te-extended to efficiently calculate Transfer Entropy for arbitrary Markov Order. Also included is a set of simple examples (we are working to expand this), and the script for the NEST simulator that we use to generate spike trains.

Also included are other causality measures for comparison:

  • xcorr, time-lagged cross-correlation
  • mi, time-lagged Mutual information
  • granger, Granger Causality (time domain)

It also contains three methods of estimating TE and GTE without binning:

  • te-binless-Leonenko, based on: L.F. Kozachenko and N.N. Leonenko, Sample Estimate of the Entropy of a Random Vector, Problems of Information Transmission, 1987
  • te-binless-Kraskov, based on: A. Kraskov et al., Estimating Mutual Information, Physical Review E, 2004
  • te-binless-Frenzel, based on: S. Frenzel and B. Pompe, Partial Mutual Information for Coupling Analysis of Multivariate Time Series, Physical Review Letters, 2007
